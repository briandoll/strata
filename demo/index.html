Let the reader choose the depth

Attention spans are shrinking. That isn't a moral failing on the reader's part — it's the water we all swim in now. A person opens twenty tabs, skims six, and finishes one. Publishers have responded with tricks: cramming the point into the first line, or writing a "TL;DR" at the top. Useful, but blunt.

Meanwhile, AI summarization has become a one-tap habit. Readers drop an article into a chatbot and read whatever comes back. It's fast, and often good enough. But that summary has two problems. It's unreviewed — the model might get the emphasis wrong, or invent a nuance you never wrote. And it isn't yours. Your voice, your framing, your judgment about what matters most: all of it is quietly handed to a third party.

Strata proposes a different arrangement. You keep writing the full piece by hand, the way you always have. Then, with an assistant's help, you draft two shorter versions: a single sentence and a paragraph or two. Crucially, you read them. You fix the emphasis, cut the line that isn't quite right, and approve them. They ship inside the same page as the full article.

The reader arrives at the full text by default — nothing is hidden, and search engines see the whole thing. If they're in a hurry, they zoom out: the article contracts to the paragraph, then to the single sentence. If that sentence catches them, they zoom back in and read the rest. It's the same gesture people already use on maps and photos, pointed at prose.

The mechanism matters less than the principle. A pinch is delightful but optional; there's always a visible control and a keyboard path, because hijacking the pinch gesture — the one low-vision readers use to enlarge text — would be a hostile thing to do carelessly. So Strata scopes its gesture to the article itself and leaves the rest of the page's zoom alone.

What you end up with is a small act of respect. You're not deciding for the reader how much of your work they'll consume. You're offering them a dial, clearly labeled, and standing behind every position on it. The short version isn't a teaser designed to trap them; it's a real, honest, author-approved version of the same idea.

There's a quieter benefit, too. Those approved summaries are structured data. A reading app, an RSS feed, or yes, someone else's AI, could consume your headline and summary instead of generating their own guess. You've written the canonical short version once, and it travels.

None of this replaces good writing. A muddy article summarized three ways is still muddy. But for work worth reading closely, giving the reader a considered way in — and a considered way out — feels like the rare interface change that's on the reader's side. That's the whole pitch. Pinch, and see.
